Academic Matters

How to Read a paper

Solve mathematical problems

  1. Identify the problem type
    • “Show that” or “Evaluate”
      • Given data and visible objective
      • deduce some statement or fact
    • “Find a …” or “Find all …”
      • Guess one answer that nearly works
      • tweak it a bit to make it more correct
      • If cannot guess one answer, you can alter the requirements
    • “Is there a …”
      • Make a decision on whether an object exists or not
      • If it exists, provide a proof.
      • If not, provide counter-example
  2. Understand the data
    • Identify the objects which satisfy some special requirements
    • Identify how the objects and requirements react to each other
    • Prepare for the proper techniques and notation
  3. Understand the objective
    • Identify the objective
      • Find an object
      • prove a statement
      • determine the existence
    • Create tactical goals
      • For example, the objective: find all the sides and angles of the triangle
      • Tactical goal: find equations involving the sides and angles of the triangle
  4. Select good notation
    • Reprensent the data and objective in notations
    • Prefer symmetrical notations
    • Trim the notation using data
  5. Write down what you know in the notation selected; draw a diagram.
    • This is an easy reference later on
    • Do not overload the paper with minutiae
    • Highlight the most useful by the relatedness to objective or data (eg, “equalities or inequalities”)
    • Put more questionable redundant, or crazy ideas in another paper
    • Draw pictures
  6. Modify the problem slightly
    • If you cannot sovle the problem directly, modify the problem slightly by
      • Consider a special case of the problem, such as extreme or degenerate cases
      • Solve a simplified version of the problem
        • Add assumptions
        • Start from most moderate assumptions
      • Formulate a conjecture which would imply the problem, and try to prove that first.
      • Derive some consequence of the problem, and try to prove that first.
      • Reformulate the problem (e.g. take the contra-positive, prove by contradiction, or try some substitution).
      • Examine solutions of similar problems.
      • Generalize the problem.
    • Get insights from the solution or technique used in solving the modified problem by observing
      • The structure of the answer
      • The structure of the solution procedurals
      • Get the feel of what strategies work
  7. Modify the problem significantly
    • Basically, we try to push the problem until it breaks
    • Identify where the breakdown
    • Removing data
    • swapping the data with the objective
    • negating the objective (e.g. trying to disprove a statement rather than prove it)

Life

Piano Practice

  1. Set the goals including:
    • Measure
    • Tempo
    • Quality in MIDI
    • Time
  2. Identify the most difficult parts and focus on them
    • Normal speed play
    • light play
    • Staccato play
  3. Diagnose the difficult parts to fundamentals
    • Hand seperate practice first, than hand together
    • If it can be solved by chord attacks, solve them by chord attacks
    • Slow practice first, then Speed up to double tempo
    • Continuity rule
    • Focus on group of fingering
    • Hand together may need more time than expected. Allocate enough time for that.
  4. Design the plan in order
  5. Do it
